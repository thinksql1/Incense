Kōdō – Rikkoku-kō

Thank you for taking part in the Kōdō Ceremony exploring the art of Rikkoku‑kō, the Six Countries of Fragrance. A special thank you to The Village Tea Hut for graciously hosting this gathering and creating a space where tradition, fragrance, and reflection could flourish. Your presence in this sanctuary of scent, poetry, and stillness brings to life a classical Japanese art of nearly one thousand years in the making. Rikkoku classification traces the subtle voices of aloeswood from different sources — six distinguished woods known as Kyara, Rakoku, Manaban, Manaka, Sasora and Sumotara — each carrying its own character and tone. In the ritual of kumikō, participants engaged in “listening” to these woods, discerning their whispers of origin and essence through delicate sensory awareness. Tonight’s ceremony invites you to wander through those six aromas, to listen deeply, to sense quietly, and to dwell in the moment where fragrance becomes a map of presence and impermanence.

The Six Categories of Rikkoku

Kyara

“gentle and dignified with a touch of bitterness, like an aristocrat in elegance and grace.”

Rakoku

“a sharp and pungent smell similar to sandalwood. Its smell is generally bitter, and reminds one of a warrior.”

Manaban

“mostly sweet; the presence of sticky oil often signals Manaban. The smell is coarse and unrefined, just like a peasant.”

Manaka

“smells light and enticing, changing like the mood of a woman with bitter feelings. None of the five qualities are easily detectable.”

Sasora

“cool and sour. Good‑quality Sasora is mistaken for Kyara, especially when it first begins to burn. It reminds one of a monk.”

Sumotara

“sour at the beginning and end. Sometimes mistaken for Kyara, but with something distasteful and ill‑bred about it, like a peasant disguised as a noble.”

Final Thought

Whether in the form of a fragrance that carries the dignity of courts, the strength of warriors, the simplicity of earth, the shift of emotion, the silence of monks, or the disguise of nobles—in listening to the six woods of Rikkoku we are invited into a space where scent becomes story, presence becomes wisdom, and the moment becomes eternity.
