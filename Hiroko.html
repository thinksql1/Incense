The Art of Gentle Warming

A beginner's guide to unlocking the subtle beauty and layered notes of aromatic botanicals.

Temperature Principles

For gentle warming practices, we use temperatures well below combustion levels to extract the pure fragrance without the smoke.

135°C

Top Notes

Start here. This gentle heat releases the delicate, fleeting top notes of the fragrance.

150-155°C

Middle Notes

When the top notes fade, turn up the heat to experience the heart and body of the aromatic.

180°C

Base Notes

Finally, increase the temperature to release the deep, grounding base notes remaining in the material.

A Note on Higher Temperatures (220°C - 260°C)

Save your used incense! You can use it later at higher temperatures approaching combustion. This will produce some smoke (though not much). Always ensure proper ventilation when working at these higher temperatures.

Tools of the Practice

Electric Incense Heater

I highly recommend this specific electric incense heater. It features a temperature dial and a timer (up to 5 hours) and comes with tweezers, a spoon, and a dish.

  • Relative Dial: The numbers on the dial do not directly reflect Celsius/Fahrenheit. Use a grill thermometer on the surface to learn the true temperature range of your specific device.
  • The Lid Effect: Placing the lid on the heater during use can significantly raise the internal temperature.

Vessels & Airflow

Properly holding your materials and directing the scent is key to a personal session.

  • Large amounts: Aluminum weigh boats are an excellent choice.
  • Personal use: Use little aluminum cone holders lined with small squares cut from food-grade aluminum foil sheets. This requires very little material, creates less waste, and makes cleanup effortless.

Hand Fan: Acquire a Japanese hand fan or a feather smudging fan to gently move the air and fragrance molecules toward you during your practice.

The Botanicals

Preparation is key. Follow these specific instructions for handling, preparing, and warming each beautiful material.

Sandalwood

Purchase an inexpensive coffee grinder specifically for woods and herbs. Grind small portions of chips and use them right away—the natural oils dry fast once ground, so fresh grinding yields the best aroma.

Frankincense

Boswellia Sacra is highly valued and perfect for beginners. Freeze the resin for 48 hours before grinding to make it less sticky. Use a separate grinder (or mortar & pestle) for resins. Clean tools with Everclear grain alcohol.

Cedar Resin

Like Frankincense, freeze the cedar resin for 48 hours before grinding to ensure a workable consistency. Always clean your grinding tools with Everclear grain alcohol afterwards.

Hinoki Cypress

Often available as stick incense. Instead of directly burning the stick, gently break it up into small pieces and place them in your aluminum dish for a smoke-free warming experience.

Jasmine

Sambac Absolute: A little goes a long way. Dip the tip of a toothpick into the bottle. Use clean fingernail clippers to snip off just the scented tip, and place that tiny piece into your warming dish so the rest of the stick doesn't get in the way.

View Source Material

Lavender

The fragrance molecules of lavender are delicate. Use gentle heat when working with Lavender powder. Alternatively, you can use Lavender essential oil applied via the toothpick method (like the Jasmine).

Labdanum

Has a sticky, black tar-like texture. Do not grind. Freeze for 48 hours, break off a pea-sized piece, and place it in the dish. This incredibly rich material can be reused for multiple warming sessions.

View Source Material

Galbanum

Extremely sticky. Do not grind. Use a silicon makeup brush to dip into the galbanum and smear a small amount directly onto the aluminum dish. Use Everclear to remove sticky residue from your brush.
