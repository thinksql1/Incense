Orientation

Safety · Expectations · Sensory Framing

Safety

Traditionally, incense practice involves combustion.

The tip of a burning incense stick can reach temperatures of approximately 800°C, producing smoke that contains carbon dioxide, carbon monoxide, aldehydes, hazardous air pollutants, and fine particulate matter (PM2.5).

These conditions can induce intoxication or altered states that are not always stable, intentional, or gentle on the nervous system.

In this practice, we do not burn incense.

Instead, we warm natural resins and aromatic materials at controlled, low temperatures. This allows beneficial volatile organic compounds to be released slowly, creating an experience that is gentle, generative, and physiologically respectful.

Nothing here is designed to overwhelm you. You remain oriented, embodied, and choiceful at all times.

Expectations

Many people purchase an electric incense warmer and contact me thinking it is broken. Why?

Because our minds are conditioned by transactional systems:

  • Push a button
  • Get an immediate result
  • Vending machine drops the candy bar

This practice does not work that way.

This is relational, not transactional.

It requires:

Willingness Uncertainty Time Attention

What you are learning here is a form of olfactory literacy — the ability to listen rather than extract.

The process is simple but subtle:

  1. Observe outwardly
  2. Name what you notice
  3. Observe what has changed in your experience
  4. Return attention gently

This is a kind of fractionated learning. You are learning by listening, not forcing.

Sensory Framing

In hypnosis and NLP, attention is often guided through multiple sensory channels:

Visual · Auditory · Kinesthetic · Gustatory · Olfactory

Engaging multiple channels can create trance, meditation, or hypnagogic states.

Most sensory information passes through the thalamus, where it is filtered, evaluated, and pattern-matched before we respond — often by referencing memory.

Olfactory perception is different.

Smell largely bypasses the thalamic relay and connects directly to the limbic system — the emotional and memory centers of the brain.

"There is no need here to disarm the critical mind. Olfactory experience does not negotiate. The scent arrives. The body responds."

02

Attention

Scent · Temperature · Subtle Shifts

Scent

Close your eyes.

Allow yourself to listen to the fragrance.

Ask gently:

  • What do I notice?
  • What is happening in my body?
  • What feels different now?

You may wish to write a few words down.

Notice the somatic response:

  • Sensations
  • Emotional tone
  • Changes in breath
  • Shifts in posture or energy

Then return attention to the fragrance and repeat.

As attention deepens, the whole body may begin to participate.

For me, this often feels like a subtle buzzing or attunement, as if the body’s frequency is aligning with the vibrating aromatic molecules.

Over time, the distinction between observer and fragrance can soften.

And finally…
become the incense.

Temperature

For personal practice, choose one resin and sit with it for a meaningful duration — 20 minutes or more.

(What begins as 20 minutes may become much longer.)

Stay below 250°C.

Most of my practice occurs between 135–150°C.

Often, unless air is gently moved toward the nose with a fan, the fragrance remains very subtle — requiring closeness and patience.

For a more precise or technical practice:

  • Review a terpene or aromatic compound profile for the resin
  • Note the temperatures at which different compounds release
  • Begin at the lowest temperature
  • Increment slowly over time

At each stage, observe:

  • Changes in scent profile
  • Visual changes in the resin
  • Memories that surface
  • Somatic responses in the body

Subtle Shifts

This is a gentle practice.

Focus more on beauty than intensity.

The experience unfolds like a journey rather than a peak.

The mind may attempt to distract you or invite multitasking.

When that happens, simply return attention to listening.

Nothing is being demanded of you.

03

Regulation

Breath · Olfaction
Inhale / Exhale

Breath

Allow your breath to slow naturally.

Simple rhythmic breathing — for example, a gentle 6-count inhale and 6-count exhale — can deepen the experience significantly.

Breath becomes:

Your drum Your rhythm Your heartbeat

Your systems begin to attune, not through control, but through rhythm.

Olfaction

The human olfactory system is capable of discriminating an enormous range of scent combinations, far beyond most other senses.

There is an important distinction here:

In synthetic fragrance, selected molecules can be combined to mimic a scent — like rose.

A natural rose, however, contains hundreds of aromatic molecules, creating a complex, living bouquet.

It’s not that synthetic fragrance is “wrong.”

It’s that natural materials carry complexity that invites longer, deeper listening.

This complexity is often perceived as alive.

04

Meaning

Embodied Memory · Insight · Presence

Embodied Memory

It is completely appropriate to pause and take notes when something reveals itself.

Write it down so you can return attention to listening.

The memories that arise may not be rational or linear.

For example:

Buttered popcorn may evoke a movie theater — that makes sense.

But why might Borneol Camphor evoke the memory of eating a fresh donut at a cider mill?

The body remembers in associative, symbolic ways.

Insight

As attention deepens, you may enter a trance-like state.

Insights can arise as:

Images Emotions Memories Felt understandings Personified sensations

Some experience this as communication with disembodied intelligence.

Others understand it as aspects of awareness that rarely have a voice.

Both frames are welcome.

Presence

Throughout all of this, the experience remains embodied.

The scent anchors the body to the physical world while awareness expands.

This can create a sense of bi-location:

Grounded in the body • Open to subtle perception

This is presence — not escape.

05

Agency

Personal Interpretation · No Doctrine

Personal Interpretation

You are developing a skill, not following a rulebook.

This unfolds over time.

If you need support, ask.

Think of this as a relationship, much like learning Tarot.

When I first learned Tarot, I was frustrated that I wasn’t meant to memorize card definitions.

Instead, I sat with each card:

  • Observed it
  • Noted what arose
  • Read the description
  • Sat with it again

Over time, each card became a container for lived memories, relationships, and understanding.

Incense works the same way.

No Doctrine

There is no single right way to practice. You develop your own relationship.

If someone wishes to explore combustion and intense altered states, that is a valid choice.
If someone wishes to explore medicinal, contemplative, or regulatory effects, that is also valid.

This practice offers options, not commandments.

"Listen rather than extract."
